Chemistry II Learning Objectives / Study Guide

Chapter 9 - Thermochemistry

  • Identify and convert between the common units of energy: joule, calorie, kilocalorie, kilowatt‐hour
  • Perform calculations involving specific heat or heat capacity
  • Describe the flow of energy, as work ($w$) or heat ($q$), from the standpoint of the system and the surroundings
  • Calculate the heat released from reactions using constant volume (bomb) calorimetry for change in internal energy ($\Delta U$) and constant pressure (coffee-cup) calorimetry for change in enthalpy ($\Delta H_{rxn}$)
  • Interpret the sign conventions associated with work, heat, and change in internal energy ($\Delta U$)
  • Distinguish between state functions and non-state functions
  • State in words and symbols the first law of thermodynamics
  • Calculate the quantity of heat released or required in a reaction based on stoichiometry given a specified quantity of reactant or product
  • Define enthalpy in terms of internal energy, pressure, and volume
  • Identify a process as endothermic or exothermic
  • Calculate enthalpy changes with respect to the stoichiometry of chemical equations
  • Describe the standard conditions for a chemical reaction
  • Define standard enthalpy of formation ($\Delta H^\circ_f$)
  • Write thermochemical equations for the formation of compounds
  • Calculate the standard enthalpy of reaction ($\Delta H^\circ_{RXN}$) using Hess’ Law
  • Calculate the standard enthalpy of reaction ($\Delta H^\circ_{RXN}$) using standard enthalpies of formation ($\Delta H^\circ_f$)
  • Estimate the enthalpy of reaction ($\Delta H^\circ_{RXN}$) using average bond energies for all bonds broken and formed in a chemical reaction
  • Use a Born‐Haber cycle to calculate the lattice energy of an ionic compound
  • Use Coulomb's law and distance between ions to rank lattice energies of ionic compounds

Chapter 12 - Thermodynamics

  • Distinguish between a spontaneous and a nonspontaneous process
  • Define entropy ($S$)
  • Recognize the conditions necessary for standard molar entropy ($S^\circ$)
  • Identify trends in standard molar entropy values
  • Calculate the standard entropy change ($\Delta S^\circ$) for a given reaction
  • Predict the sign of $\Delta S$ for a given process and use the sign to indicate whether the system has undergone an increase or decrease in entropy
  • Calculate $\Delta S_{surr}$ given $\Delta H_{sys}$ and temperature
  • Determine whether a process is spontaneous given $\Delta S_{surr}$ and $\Delta S_{sys}$
  • State in words or symbols the second law and third law of thermodynamics
  • Define Gibbs free energy ($G$)
  • Calculate $\Delta G$ from temperature, $\Delta H$, and $\Delta S$
  • Use the sign of $\Delta G$ to indicate if a process is spontaneous
  • Predict the sign of $\Delta G$ given the signs of $\Delta H$ and $\Delta S$ at high and low temperatures
  • Recognize the conditions necessary for standard free energy of formation ($\Delta G^\circ_f$)
  • Calculate the standard free energy change ($\Delta G^\circ$) of a given reaction
  • Calculate the temperature at which a process becomes spontaneous

Chapter 10 - Liquids and Solids

  • Define and identify intermolecular forces
  • Describe the physical properties of liquids (surface tension, viscosity, vapor pressure, boiling point)
  • Describe the relationship between vapor pressure and temperature
  • Describe the physical properties of solids (melting point, vapor pressure, and amorphous vs. crystalline structures)
  • Describe the basic types of crystalline solids (ionic, covalent, molecular, metallic)
  • Identify phase changes and their associated enthalpies
  • Interpret heating/cooling curves
  • Calculate heat ($q$) changes with temperature and/or phase changes
  • Interpret a phase diagram

Chapter 11 – Solutions and Colloids

  • Describe types of solutions, including all phases
  • Predict if a solution will form based on intermolecular forces
  • Describe saturation states of a solution (saturated, unsaturated, supersaturated)
  • Calculate concentration in a variety of ways (molarity, molality, and %mass vs % by volume)
  • Use concentration to perform stoichiometric calculations
  • Describe how environmental factors (temperature and pressure) affect solubility
  • Explain and calculate different physical properties between solutions and pure solvent (colligative properties)
  • vapor pressure lowering
  • boiling point elevation
  • freezing point depression
  • osmotic pressure
  • Use colligative properties to estimate molar mass
  • Calculate and explain colligative properties of strong electrolytes (van’t Hoff factor)

Chapter 13 - Fundamental Equilibrium Concepts

  • Define equilibrium
  • Predict the shift of a reaction using Le Châtelier’s principle given a change in one of the following: removal or addition of reactant or product, change in volume or pressure, and temperature change
  • Write the equilibrium constant ($K$) expression for a given reaction
  • Determine the resulting equilibrium constant ($K$) if the equation for a chemical reaction is reversed, multiplied, added to another equation and/or any combination of these changes
  • Explain the relationship between the equilibrium constant ($K$) and the reaction quotient ($Q$)
  • Calculate equilibrium constant ($K$) given equilibrium concentrations of reactants and products
  • Predict the relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium given the equilibrium constant ($K$)
  • Calculate the equilibrium concentration of reactants or products given initial concentrations
  • Differentiate between heterogeneous and homogeneous equilibria
  • Convert between $K_c$ and $K_p$ for a reaction involving gases
  • Calculate the reaction quotient ($Q$) and predict the direction of a reaction given initial concentrations of reactants and products and the equilibrium constant ($K$)
  • Use an ICE table and, if necessary, the quadratic formula to determine equilibrium, initial or final concentrations of reactants and products
  • Employ and justify the “$x$ is small” approximation using the 5% rule
  • Calculate $\Delta G$ given $\Delta G^\circ$ and $Q$ at a specified temperature and predict the spontaneous direction of reaction
  • Calculate $K$ given $\Delta G^\circ$ at a specified temperature

Chapter 14 - Acid Base Equilibria

  • Write base/conjugate acid and acid/conjugate base pairs
  • Define through words and examples Brønsted acids/bases
  • Define amphoteric and give examples of substances that are amphoteric
  • Determine the relative strength of acids based on their composition and structure
  • Write the equilibrium expression for water ($K_w$) and use it to determine whether a solution is acidic, basic, or neutral
  • Classify a solution as being acidic, basic, or neutral using the pH scale
  • Calculate pH, pOH, concentration of hydroxide ion, or concentration of protons
  • Identify an acid or base as being strong or weak
  • Calculate the pH of a strong acid or base
  • Calculate the pH of a weak acid or base using an ICE table, the acid or base-dissociation constant ($K_a$ or $K_b$) and, if necessary, the quadratic equation
  • Employ the “$x$ is small” approximation and justify assumption
  • Explain the relationship between $K_a$, $K_b$, and $K_w$
  • Calculate the percent ionization of a weak acid or base
  • Use the pH of a weak acid or base solution to calculate the $K_a$ or $K_b$, respectively
  • Calculate the $K_a$ or $K_b$ of conjugate pairs using $K_w$
  • Identify polyprotic acids and calculate their pH at a given concentration
  • Classify a salt as being basic, acidic, or neutral based upon the acid and base used to form the salt
  • Calculate the pH of a salt solution

Buffers

  • Recognize that a solution containing a weak acid and a salt containing its conjugate base is a buffer solution
  • Describe how a buffer can neutralize small amounts of strong acid or strong base
  • Calculate the pH of a buffer
  • Calculate the pH of a buffer after the addition of a small amount of strong acid or base
  • Select an appropriate acid and salt to prepare a buffer of a specific pH and calculate the necessary ratio of Buffer Acid to Buffer Base
  • Explain buffer capacity and range

Titration

  • Predict whether the products of an acid-base neutralization (titration) will be acidic, basic or neutral
  • Describe the different profiles of titration curves depending upon the acid and base strengths
  • Describe the species present in different regions of titration curves depending upon the acid and base strengths
  • Identify and explain the buffer region of a titration curve if it is present

Chapter 15 – Equilibria of Other Reaction Classes

  • Identify Lewis acids and bases
  • Write $K_{sp}$ expressions given the name or formula of a sparingly soluble salt
  • Calculate molar solubility from $K_{sp}$ values and vice versa
  • Calculate molar solubility from $K_{sp}$ values in the presence of a common ion
  • Use $K_{sp}$ values to predict whether precipitation will occur
  • Know how factors such as a common ion or pH will affect solubility of a specific salt
  • Write equilibrium expressions for formation of complex ions ($K_f$)
  • Describe the effect of complex ion formation on solubility of metal ions

Chapter 16 - Electrochemistry

  • Define oxidation, reduction, and oxidation‐reduction (redox) reactions
  • Assign oxidation states to atoms in a chemical formula
  • Identify the elements undergoing oxidation and reduction in a redox reaction
  • Use oxidation number rules to determine the oxidation number of each element in a compound or polyatomic ion
  • Identify the element being oxidized and the element being reduced in an oxidation-reduction reaction
  • Identify the reducing agent and the oxidizing agent in an oxidation-reduction reaction
  • Classify combustion reactions as oxidation-reduction
  • Balance oxidation-reduction reactions taking place in neutral, acidic or basic solutions
  • Predict the direction of the flow of electrons, identify the anode and cathode, and calculate the cell potential for a voltaic (galvanic) cell
  • Write line notation for a voltaic cell
  • Identify the various components of a voltaic cell from the line notation
  • Recognize the role of the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) in defining the zero point for the standard cell potential
  • Given two half cells, predict the spontaneous reaction, and calculate the standard cell potential ($E^\circ$), free energy ($\Delta G$) and the equilibrium constant ($K$)
  • Use the Nernst equation to calculate cell potentials and unknown electrolyte concentrations under non-standard conditions
  • Explain the operation and driving force of concentration cells
  • Differentiate between a voltaic and electrolytic cell

Chapter 17 - Kinetics

  • Describe the factors (concentration, temperature, catalyst) that affect the rate of reactions, and describe how these factors affect reaction rates
  • Determine the instantaneous rate of a reaction given a graph of concentration versus time
  • Express the rate of a reaction in terms of rate of loss of reactants or rate of formation of products given the balanced chemical reaction
  • Write rate laws for first-order, zero-order and second-order reactions
  • Determine the rate law and calculate the rate constant for a reaction using the method of initial rates
  • Use graphical treatment of kinetic data to determine reaction order, rate law, and the rate constant
  • Use integrated rate laws to calculate the concentration of reactant at a given time
  • Use half-life and reaction order to calculate the concentration of reactant at a given time
  • Use the Arrhenius equation or an Arrhenius plot ($\ln k$ vs. $1/T$) to determine activation energy of a reaction
  • Interpret reaction profile (energy vs. reaction progress) graphs to find the activation energy of forward and reverse reactions
  • Given several reaction mechanisms, be able to determine which are consistent with reaction profile (energy vs. reaction progress), the stoichiometry, and rate law for the reaction
  • List the criteria for a proposed mechanism to be plausible
  • Describe how a catalyst (or an inhibitor) affects reaction rates
  • Identify reaction intermediates in a given reaction mechanism

Chapter 20 - Nuclear Chemistry

  • Distinguish between chemical reactions and nuclear reactions
  • Describe the properties of alpha, beta, neutron and positron particles and gamma rays
  • Describe the different types of radioactive decay
  • Write balanced nuclear reactions
  • List the factors that contribute to nuclear instability
  • Apply 1st order kinetics to nuclear decay: use half-life to determine the age of an object
  • Calculate nuclear binding energy from masses of atoms
  • Distinguish between nuclear fusion and nuclear fission
